# Dead Zone: A Vibe Coding Case Study
### By a 6th Grader Who Built a Game With AI

---

## What Even Is Vibe Coding?

Vibe coding is when you build something with AI by describing what you want in plain language instead of writing the code yourself. You're the creative director — you have the vision, the ideas, and the decisions. The AI handles the technical side. You don't need to know how to code. You just need to know what you want.

---

## How It Started

I was watching YouTube one day and came across a video about building a game using AI. I thought — I could actually try that. I didn't have a grand plan or a design document. I just knew I wanted to make a zombie shooter, opened up Claude, and started describing what I had in my head.

That's the thing about vibe coding. You don't start with code. You start with an idea.

---

## The Map Moment

If I had to pick one moment where I knew this project was going to be something real, it was when I described the map.

I told Claude I wanted an abandoned city feel — broken concrete, abandoned cars you could use as cover, dumpster fires burning in the background. I had this picture in my head of a place that felt dangerous and lived-in, like something bad had already happened here before you even showed up.

And when it rendered? The cars were there. The dumpster fires were flickering with layered flame animations. There was graffiti on the ground that said things like NO HOPE and DEAD END. It looked exactly like what I was imagining — maybe even better.

That was the moment I thought: *okay, this is going to be a lot more than I expected.*

---

## Building It Out — The Big Decisions

From there the game grew piece by piece, and almost every expansion came from a simple question: *what would make this more interesting?*

**Adding enemy variety** was one of the first big decisions. Normal zombies were fine, but one type of enemy gets old fast. I added fungi zombies — slow, bloated, hits hard — and electric zombies that sprint at you and shock you on contact. Each type forced the player to think differently. The electric ones in particular changed how you move, because you can't just stand still and shoot anymore.

**The wave structure** gave the game a sense of progression. Waves 1–4 are just normal zombies, letting new players get comfortable. Wave 5 introduces fungi. Wave 7 brings in electric. Each wave feels like a new challenge unlocking rather than the same thing getting harder.

**The boss** was the biggest creative leap. I didn't just want a big zombie — I wanted something that felt like a final encounter, something genuinely threatening. The Mutant Zombie King has four different abilities, an entrance sequence where it erupts from the ground, a death pit that stays on the map and kills you if you fall in, and its own horror death screen if it kills you. Designing something that layered took a lot of back and forth, but every piece of it was a decision I made about what would feel the most intense and memorable.

**The horror death screen** was one of my favorite additions. When the boss kills you, the screen flickers black with red static, then fades to pure darkness, and a description appears of how the King crushed you. There are four different messages that rotate randomly. That detail didn't need to be there — the game would work fine without it. But it makes dying to the boss feel different from dying to a regular zombie, and that matters.

**The password-locked wave panel** was a practical idea that turned into a fun feature. I wanted a way to jump to any wave for testing and showing people specific parts of the game. Adding a password — `0l1v3r` — made it feel like a secret rather than just a debug tool. And the fact that it re-locks every time you die means it stays hidden unless you know it's there.

---

## Claude vs. ChatGPT

I actually tried building this with ChatGPT first. A lot of the time when I described something new, it would crash the game and I'd have to go back and fix the broken code before I could move forward. It slowed everything down and was honestly pretty frustrating.

With Claude, that didn't happen once. Every time I described a new feature — a new enemy type, a new boss ability, a new UI element — it integrated cleanly with everything already there. The game kept working and kept growing. That reliability was a big deal. It meant I could focus on the creative decisions instead of constantly putting out fires.

---

## What I'd Add Next

If I kept going, I'd basically build a whole second chapter. A level two with a new map, new zombie types, new boss, and new abilities. I'd make the shotgun a real weapon you can use instead of just a win condition. It would almost feel like a new game built on top of the first one.

The foundation is already there. That's the thing about building this way — when the base is solid, expanding it is the fun part.

---

## What I Learned

Vibe coding taught me that having a clear vision matters more than knowing how to code. Every good thing in this game came from a specific decision I made — not from the AI guessing what I wanted, but from me knowing what I wanted and describing it well.

The map, the boss, the horror death screen, the password, the enemy variety — those were all my ideas. Claude built them. But the game is mine.

That's what vibe coding is. It's not cheating. It's a new way to make things.
